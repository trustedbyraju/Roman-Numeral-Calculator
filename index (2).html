Introduction to Roman Numerals

Roman numerals are a numeral system that originated in ancient Rome and remained the dominant numbering system throughout Europe well into the Late Middle Ages. Unlike the decimal system we use today, Roman numerals are based on combinations of seven Latin letters: I, V, X, L, C, D, and M. You still encounter them regularly — on clock faces, book chapter headings, film copyright dates, and sports events like the Super Bowl.

How the Roman Numeral Calculator Works

Our Roman Numeral Calculator uses a precise parsing algorithm. When you enter a Roman numeral, the tool reads each character left to right. If a smaller value appears before a larger value (e.g., IV), it is subtracted; otherwise it is added. This correctly handles all standard subtractive notation rules.

For the Number to Roman Converter, the algorithm works top-down: it divides your number by the largest Roman value that fits, appends the symbol, then repeats with the remainder — a greedy approach that always produces the shortest valid representation.

What is the largest number this calculator supports? +
The standard Roman numeral system supports values from 1 to 3999 (I to MMMCMXCIX). Numbers outside this range cannot be represented with classical Roman numerals.
What are the seven Roman numeral symbols? +
The seven symbols are: I (1), V (5), X (10), L (50), C (100), D (500), and M (1000). All Roman numerals are built from combinations of these letters.
Why is there no zero in Roman numerals? +
Roman numerals were developed without a concept of zero. The system represents quantities additively and subtractively, so zero has no natural place in the notation. The concept of zero was later introduced through Indian and Arabic mathematics.
What does XIV mean in Roman numerals? +
XIV equals 14. X = 10 and IV = 4 (5 minus 1 using subtractive notation), so X + IV = 14.
